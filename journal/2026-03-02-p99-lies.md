# Why p99 lies, and what to track instead

p99 is a single number pretending to be a distribution. It's useful as a smoke detector. It is **not** useful as a decision-making tool, and I've watched teams wreck their roadmaps chasing a p99 number that was hiding three completely different failure modes.

## What p99 hides

When your p99 is 950ms, that could mean:

1. Everything is uniformly slow at 950ms. (Capacity problem.)
2. 99% of requests are at 200ms, 1% hit a 5s timeout. (Dependency problem.)
3. 99% of requests are at 200ms, 1% hit a 10s GC pause. (JVM problem.)

Three completely different investigations. One number.

## What to track instead

For anything p99-sensitive, track:

- **The histogram**, not the summary. Ship it to Datadog as a distribution metric.
- **p50 vs p99 ratio.** A healthy service has them close together. A divergence means tail pathology.
- **Saturation of every downstream.** Tail latency almost always comes from something you don't own.
- **The cost of a retry.** If retries are cheap and idempotent, slow tails stop mattering.

## The operational shift

We stopped setting p99 SLOs as hard targets and started setting *shape* SLOs: "p99/p50 ratio < 3x." That single change surfaced more real problems than six months of p99-chasing.
